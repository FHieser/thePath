# Path Network Mechanics v2

**A Path** is a combination of locations that form a way through the mist together. Locations themselves are isolated environments that have their own unique theme and interactable stats. The goal of this document is to create a guide on how to create and use the mechanics of a path and its locations.

Creating a path requires three essential elements: location cards to represent each environment,  a grid to organize the journey, and a placement method to populate the grid. The grid provides a coordinate system that maps both the current path and tracks stable locations between sessions. Location cards are the building blocks—each one represents a specific environment with its own mechanics and narrative hooks. The placement method determines how these cards get onto the grid. Together, these elements create a flexible system that supports both structured storytelling and emergent exploration.

## Path locations

Each location in a Path is represented by a card. These cards can be physical tarot cards, simple pieces of paper, or any token system that works for your table. It's recommended to keep the symbols on these cards abstract, as players shouldn't immediately know which location they represent. This mystery encourages players to track their discoveries themselves, increasing engagement and maintaining the campaign's sense of exploration.

Locations are based on Daggerheart environments (see SRD for base environment rules). Each location in this campaign frame includes modifiers that affect how it interacts with the Path system—properties like Stable, Underground, Cave Mouth, or Mist-Touched that change how the location behaves during play. The Modifiers section below describes each of these in detail.

**Example Card Mappings:**
When using tarot cards, select cards based on visual fit and thematic resonance. Major Arcana work well for significant locations like villages or sacred sites ("10 Wheel of Fortune" = Village in the Mist, "18 The Moon" = Sacred Mountain Pass). Minor Arcana can follow suit-based themes: Wands for natural open areas (2 of Wands = Windswept Grasslands), Goblets for structures and crossings (Ace of Goblets = Trading Grounds, 10 of Goblets = Ancient Crossing).

## Grid System

The grid maps where locations appear in relation to each other. It serves two purposes: tracking the current Path for this session, and remembering where Stable locations sit between sessions. The grid has no fixed size—it expands as players discover new areas. You'll use separate grids for surface and underground locations, with Cave Mouth locations connecting between the two layers.

The coordinate format is Row | Column (e.g., A1, B3, A|-3). Rows use letters (A, B, C, etc.) and columns use numbers (1, 2, 3, etc.). Negative columns track west expansion (A|-3, B|-1), and inverse rows mark opposite sides (A', B'). The grid has no fixed boundaries—it expands as needed.

Stable locations maintain their grid position between sessions and cannot disappear into the mist. These locations persist on the grid whether or not they appear on the current Path. They may be naturally stable (ancient sites, Sacred Flame sanctuaries) or stabilized by players using Flame Essence rituals. Unstable locations disappear between sessions and may reappear elsewhere. Most new locations start as Unstable.

Underground locations exist on a separate underground grid below the surface. They can only connect to other Underground locations or to surface locations with the Cave Mouth modifier. Examples include Mining Complex, Geothermal Caverns, and Sacred Underground.

With the grid structure established, you need a method to decide which locations appear and where they're placed each session.

## Placement Method

There are multiple ways to populate the grid with location cards:

**Deliberate Placement:** The GM selects which locations to place and where they appear on the grid. This gives full control over story structure, encounter difficulty, and narrative pacing. Recommended for early sessions and story-critical moments.

**Random Draw:** The GM shuffles the location deck and draws cards randomly one by one. For each card drawn, the GM decides where to place it on the grid face-down. This creates emergent gameplay while still giving the GM control over Path layout and connectivity. Later in the campaign, you can introduce player participation through village projects or character progression—players draw a set number of location cards (based on their upgrade level), preview them, and decide where to place them on the grid. This adds strategic choice where players can optimize their route based on what they draw.

When placing locations on the grid, all locations except previously-visited Stable locations start face-down. Players discover what awaits them as they travel the Path.

**Recommendation for New Campaigns:** When starting out with this campaign frame, use the Deliberate Placement method. A good first session is a short Path with 4-6 surface locations that go from one Flame-Touched location to the next. In subsequent sessions, introduce Cave Mouth locations and underground areas, and allow for branching paths where players choose between multiple routes. When you feel comfortable with the system, switch over to Random Draw if you want to add more emergent surprises to your game.



## Example Path Creation

Creating a Path involves several steps that you'll follow each session. Here's the process:

**Step 1: Determine Your Starting Point**
Decide where players begin this session. Usually this is either Village in the Mist or the location where the previous session ended.

**Step 2: Decide on Path Length**
Choose how many locations will appear on this Path. For early sessions, 4-6 locations work well. Longer campaigns can have 6-10 locations per Path.

**Step 3: Select Your Placement Method**
Use either Deliberate Placement (you choose which locations) or Random Draw (shuffle and draw cards). For first sessions, Deliberate Placement is recommended.

**Step 4: Choose Your Locations**
Based on your placement method, select which location cards will appear. Consider story needs, challenge level, and which modifiers you want to introduce.

**Step 5: Place Locations on the Grid**
Position your selected locations on the grid, deciding their coordinates and how they connect. Place Stable locations in their known positions if they've been visited before. Place all other locations face-down.

**Step 6: Add Underground Locations (Optional)**
If you want underground exploration, place Underground locations on the separate underground grid and ensure some surface locations have the Cave Mouth modifier for access.

**Step 7: Review Connections**
Check that the Path makes sense—can players reach their destination? Are there interesting route choices? Do Cave Mouth locations properly connect surface and underground?

**Session 1 Example - Starting Simple:**

Players begin their first session with a straightforward linear Path:

**Surface Grid:**
```
| Location | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Village in the Mist | Stone Works | Ancient Crossing | Trading Grounds | Sacred Mountain Pass |
```

This simple Path introduces the core mechanics without overwhelming new players. All five locations are on the surface in a single row, creating a clear journey from the starting village (A1) to another Flame-Touched sanctuary (A5). Players travel east, encountering three locations along the way. No branching paths or underground access yet—just a direct route to learn the basics.

**Session 2 Example - Adding Complexity:**

Once players understand the basics, introduce branching paths and underground locations:

**Surface Grid:**
```
| Location | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Village in the Mist | - | - | - | Sacred Mountain Pass |
| B | Stone Works | Ancient Crossing | Trading Grounds | Windswept Grasslands | Highland Watch |
```

**Underground Grid:**
```
| Location | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | - | - | - | - | - |
| B | Mining Complex | Geothermal Caverns | Sacred Underground | - | - |
```

Now players have choices. Starting from Village in the Mist (A1), they must travel south to Stone Works (B1) to continue their journey. From there, they can follow the surface Path east through Ancient Crossing, Trading Grounds, and Windswept Grasslands toward Highland Watch. Alternatively, Cave Mouth modifiers on locations like Stone Works or Ancient Crossing provide access to the underground network—players can descend into Mining Complex and travel through Geothermal Caverns and Sacred Underground as an alternate route. This introduces vertical exploration and meaningful route decisions.

## Modifiers

Modifiers are properties that change how a location functions within the Path system. Some modifiers affect grid behavior (Stable, Underground) while others add environmental features, encounters, or hazards. Each location can have multiple modifiers that work together.

**Location Persistence Modifiers**

These modifiers control whether locations remain fixed on the grid or shift with the mist between sessions. This mechanic creates the dynamic, ever-changing world of the Path—most locations are temporary and may never appear in the same configuration twice, while a few stable anchor points provide continuity and allow players to build lasting connections to specific places.

**Unstable:** The location disappears into the mist between sessions and may reappear elsewhere on future Paths. Most new locations start as Unstable. When a session ends, remove Unstable location cards from the grid entirely—they only exist when part of the current Path. These locations represent areas where the mist's grip is strong and reality shifts constantly. Players can convert Unstable locations to Stable through campaign progression, such as placing a Sacred Flame or establishing a permanent outpost.

**Stable:** The location maintains its relative grid position and connections between sessions and will not disappear into the mist. The location persists on the grid whether or not it appears on the current Path, allowing players to navigate back to known locations. Locations may be naturally Stable due to ancient magical anchors or Sacred Flame sanctuaries, or they can be stabilized by players through specific campaign achievements like establishing a new Sacred Flame or completing faction quests. Examples include Village in the Mist and Sacred Mountain Pass.

**Vertical Navigation Modifiers**

These modifiers create a two-layer world with surface and underground locations. They enable vertical exploration and allow players to choose between surface routes and subterranean paths.

**Cave Mouth:** The location contains an entrance to underground areas—a natural cave opening, artificial mine shaft, or other descent point. This modifier connects the surface grid to the underground grid, allowing players to travel between layers. A surface location with Cave Mouth can link to any adjacent Underground location on the underground grid. Examples include Stone Works (mine entrance) and Silken Spire (silk-wrapped descent shaft).

**Underground:** The location exists on the separate underground grid below the surface. Underground locations can only connect to other Underground locations or to surface locations that have the Cave Mouth modifier. These locations represent caves, tunnels, underground rivers, and subterranean chambers. Players cannot access Underground locations without first finding a Cave Mouth entrance. Examples include Mining Complex, Geothermal Caverns, and Sacred Underground.

*Note: This vertical layer system could theoretically extend to additional dimensions beyond surface and underground. You could add sky-level locations accessible via climbing or flight, deeper underground layers beyond the first subterranean level, or even parallel dimensions like the Upside Down from Stranger Things or the Otherworld from Silent Hill. Each would use its own grid with specific connection modifiers to link between layers.*
**Corruption & Protection Modifiers**

These modifiers represent the eternal struggle between the Sacred Flames that hold back the mist and the corrupting influence of the mist itself. They fundamentally alter the danger level and resource availability of a location and have special interaction rules (see Mist Mechanics section).

**Flame-Touched:** The location contains a Sacred Flame that provides protection from the mist. This modifier always comes paired with the Stable modifier—Sacred Flames cannot disappear into the mist and serve as permanent anchor points for navigation. Players can harvest Flame Essence from these locations to fuel village projects or establish new Sacred Flames at other locations. Flame-Touched locations are safe havens where the mist cannot corrupt players or spawn mist adversaries. Examples include Village in the Mist and Sacred Mountain Pass. Some locations can be converted to Flame-Touched through player actions, such as successfully establishing a new Sacred Flame through specific quests or rituals.

**Mist-Touched:** The location has been corrupted by prolonged mist exposure and reality warps under the mist's influence. These locations always contain mist adversary encounters, can distribute Mist tokens to players who linger too long, and feature unstable terrain affected by reality distortions. The GM can spend Fear to make locations Mist-Touched during play (see Mist Mechanics section).

**Environmental & Encounter Modifiers**

These modifiers add specific features, inhabitants, or encounter types to a location. They help define what players will find when they arrive and what challenges or opportunities await them.

**Wild Beasts:** The location always contains an encounter with wild beasts—predators, territorial creatures, or dangerous wildlife. These encounters reflect the local ecosystem and can range from wolves and bears on the surface to giant cave spiders or subterranean predators underground. Wild beast encounters provide hunting opportunities for resources, create navigation hazards that must be overcome or avoided, and add natural danger separate from the mist's corruption.

**Inhabited:** The location has NPCs present—travelers, refugees, traders, or other communities. This creates roleplay opportunities, introduces potential allies or enemies, and provides access to trade, information, or faction interactions. Inhabited locations might contain friendly settlements, hostile bandit camps, or neutral trading posts. The specific inhabitants should reflect the location's environment and position relative to the Path.


**Special Placement Modifiers**

These modifiers define unique grid placement behaviors and structural connections between locations. They control how locations must be positioned during Path generation and which other locations they depend on.

**Routes(X):** This location must connect to exactly X adjacent locations on the grid. The number determines the location's role in the Path network:
- **Routes(1)** - Dead-end location that connects to only 1 adjacent location. Perfect for side-branches, isolated outposts, or terminal destinations that don't lead onward. Example: Silken Spire appears as an optional detour visible from the main Path but leads nowhere beyond itself.
- **Routes(2)** - Linear passage that connects to exactly 2 adjacent locations. The location sits between two other locations, creating a mandatory waypoint or bridge. Most standard Path locations don't specify Routes, meaning they can connect to any number of neighbors.
- **Routes(3)** - Junction point that connects to exactly 3 adjacent locations, creating a fork where players choose between multiple routes.
- **Routes(4)** - Crossroads or hub that connects to exactly 4 adjacent locations, forming a central meeting point for multiple Path branches.

During Path generation, when placing a location with Routes(X), ensure it connects to exactly X other locations. If using Random Draw placement, you may need to adjust the grid layout to satisfy these constraints.

**Linked(Location Name):** This location always appears together with the specified location(s) when drawn during Path generation. Drawing either card brings both locations into play as a linked set. The specific connection rules and mechanical interactions between linked locations are defined in their Features sections—examine those features to understand how the locations relate (shared Stable/Unstable state, Cave Mouth connections, narrative ties, etc.). Example: Silken Spire has Linked(Brood Throne). The two locations share the same Unstable state, and a Cave Mouth descent shaft connects the surface fortress to the underground throne chamber below. Stabilizing one stabilizes both.

## Session Tracking

The Path campaign creates persistent world state that evolves based on player actions. Between sessions, track several types of information to maintain continuity and ensure player choices have lasting consequences.

**Location Cards:** Record which physical cards represent which locations in your campaign. This mapping stays consistent throughout the campaign—once you assign a card to a location, it always represents that location. Keep this reference accessible so you and your players can identify locations when cards are drawn or placed on the grid. See the Path Locations section above for card mapping examples. Example:
- "10 of Goblets" = Ancient Crossing
- "2 of Wands" = Windswept Grasslands

**Grid and Location Persistence:** The grid serves as your campaign's persistent geography—a map of the world your players are discovering. Each session's Path is built on top of this foundation, with Stable locations providing familiar landmarks and safe harbors. Start each new session by laying out your existing Stable location grid, then add new locations around them to create the current Path.

Between sessions, maintain your grid map showing all location coordinates for both surface and underground grids. Stable locations remain in their grid positions whether or not they appear on the current Path—these are permanent fixtures of your world. Unstable locations disappear from the grid entirely when the session ends—remove their cards and return them to the deck for future random draws.

**Example - Session 3 Grid:**

**Surface Grid:**
```
| Location | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|----------|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Village in the Mist (Stable) | - | - | - | Sacred Mountain Pass (Stable) |
| B | - | - | - | - | - |
| C | - | - | Silken Spire (Stable) | - | - |
```

**Underground Grid:**
```
| Location | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| A | - | - | - |
| B | - | - | - |
| C | - | - | Brood Throne (Stable) |
```

At the start of Session 3, these four Stable locations form your foundation.

**Example - Session 4 Grid (After Players Stabilize Trading Grounds):**

**Surface Grid:**
```
| Location | -3 | -2 | -1 | 1 | 2 | 3 | 4 | 5 |
|----------|----|----|-------|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Trading Grounds (Stable) | - | - | Village in the Mist (Stable) | - | - | - | Sacred Mountain Pass (Stable) |
| B | - | - | - | - | - | - | - | - |
| C | - | - | - | - | - | Silken Spire (Stable) | - | - |
```

**Underground Grid:**
```
| Location | 1 | 2 | 3 |
|----------|---|---|---|
| A | - | - | - |
| B | - | - | - |
| C | - | - | Brood Throne (Stable) |
```

During Session 3, players successfully stabilized Trading Grounds at coordinate A|-3. The grid expanded westward to accommodate this new permanent location. Notice how negative columns (-3, -2, -1) now appear—the grid grows organically as players explore and stabilize new areas. At the start of Session 4, you'll build the new Path using these five Stable locations as anchor points.

**Location Counters:** Many location features use counters to track ongoing situations—a crumbling bridge deteriorating over time, a village project nearing completion, or accumulated disturbances in a dangerous area. Record the current value of each counter when the session ends. When players return to that location, the counter resumes from its last known state. The mechanics that introduce each counter explain how and when it advances—some progress automatically between sessions, others only change through player actions. Example:
- Ancient Crossing - Crumbling Bridge counter: 5/7 (bridge halfway collapsed). The Crumbling Bridge feature advances when players cross the bridge or when heavy weight is placed on it.
- Village in the Mist - Extend Cooking Station counter: 3 days remaining

**NPC Positions:** Track where important NPCs are located and their current status. NPCs can move between locations based on player actions or story developments. Record their current location, their attitude toward the party, and any significant events involving them. Example:
- The Watcher - Located in Forest Refuge (moved there after players cleared marauders from his dwelling). Friendly to the party.
- Factionless Leader - Last seen at Trading Grounds. Hostile (survived combat encounter with party).

## Mist Mechanics

The mist is more than atmospheric scenery—it's an active threat that corrupts those who venture into its grasp. This section covers three interconnected mechanics: Mist-Touched locations that pose immediate dangers, off-Path travel that offers desperate gambles, and Mist tokens that track accumulating corruption.

These mechanics create natural boundaries and consequences. Players learn to respect corrupted areas, plan their rest carefully, and weigh risks against rewards.

### Mist-Touched Locations

Locations become Mist-Touched when the corrupting mist gains a strong foothold. Thick supernatural fog visibly shrouds these places. The GM can spend Fear to make locations Mist-Touched during play (see Fear Spending Options below), certain adversary abilities can create temporary Mist-Touched zones, and locations may start Mist-Touched due to story circumstances. Critically, **all locations not on the current Path are Mist-Touched**—the world beyond the established route is consumed by corruption.

Mist-Touched locations reduce visibility and create an atmosphere of dread. The GM may create mist adversary encounters when appropriate—creatures like Mist Walkers, Mist Stalkers, Wraith Leaders, or other corrupted beings. Match encounter composition to the party's tier and narrative situation. Not every Mist-Touched location requires combat, but the threat is always present.

The greatest danger comes from resting in corrupted areas. Characters who take a short rest in a Mist-Touched location gain 2 Mist tokens. Those desperate enough to attempt a long rest gain 3 Mist tokens. This creates meaningful tension—players must weigh recovery against accumulating corruption. Sometimes pushing forward exhausted is safer than stopping to heal.

After players defeat encounters or perform cleansing rituals, the GM may remove the Mist-Touched status. This rewards players for actively fighting back against the mist rather than simply enduring it.

**Mist-Touched Location Rules (Quick Reference):**

**How locations become Mist-Touched:**
- GM spends Fear (see Fear Spending Options below)
- Certain adversary abilities create temporary Mist-Touched zones
- Locations may start Mist-Touched due to story circumstances
- **All locations not on the current Path are Mist-Touched**

**Effects:**
- **Visibility**: Thick supernatural fog reduces visibility and creates atmosphere of dread
- **Encounters**: GM may create mist adversary encounters appropriate to tier and situation
- **Rest Penalties**:
  - Short Rest → Each character gains 2 Mist tokens
  - Long Rest → Each character gains 3 Mist tokens
- **GM Discretion**: After defeating encounters or cleansing rituals, GM may remove Mist-Touched status

### Off-Path Travel

Sometimes players need to leave the established Path—perhaps they're lost, fleeing danger, or attempting a desperate shortcut. Off-Path travel represents venturing into uncharted mist where reality shifts and direction becomes meaningless. This is dangerous and unpredictable by design.

When players leave the Path, shuffle all Unstable location cards and begin drawing randomly. Each time players move, draw a location card from the deck—including Underground locations that might represent sudden chasms or cave systems. Place only this single card on the table with empty space around it. This physical presentation emphasizes the isolation and disorientation—they're unmoored from the grid, drifting through corrupted space.

As players move forward, remove the previous location card. It vanishes back into the mist, cutting off retreat. They can only press onward, drawing new locations and hoping to find salvation. If players draw a Stable location, they emerge from the mist at that location's grid coordinates—back on known ground.

All off-Path locations are Mist-Touched, carrying all the dangers above—possible mist adversary encounters and harsh rest penalties. Additionally, the GM can spend Fear more cheaply to corrupt the party while off-Path (2 Fear instead of 3), representing the mist's stronger grip in uncharted areas. Off-Path travel is a last resort gamble.

**Off-Path Travel Procedure (Quick Reference):**

1. **Shuffle the location deck** - Mix all Unstable location cards together
2. **Draw randomly for each new location** - Draw a location card from the deck (including Underground locations)
3. **Place only the current location** - Put only this single card on the table with empty space around it
4. **Previous location vanishes** - When players move forward, remove the previous location card
5. **Escape condition** - If players draw a **Stable location**, they emerge at that location's grid coordinates

**Remember**: All off-Path locations are Mist-Touched (possible encounters, rest penalties apply, GM spends 2 Fear instead of 3 to corrupt party)

### Mist Corruption

The mist corrupts those exposed to its influence. Characters accumulate Mist tokens through specific triggers, and the corruption worsens with each token gained. This system tracks the mist's gradual claim on a character, creating escalating consequences that eventually become fatal.

Characters gain Mist tokens from intentional triggers, keeping each gain dramatic and tied to meaningful choices. Resting in Mist-Touched locations is the most common source—2 tokens for a short rest, 3 for a long rest. Certain mist creatures inflict Mist tokens through attacks or special abilities. The GM can spend Fear to give every party member 1 Mist token (3 Fear normally, 2 Fear when off-Path). Notably, characters do not gain tokens simply from entering or traveling through Mist-Touched locations—the corruption requires sustained exposure or direct contact with mist creatures.

When a character gains a Mist token, they immediately suffer consequences based on their total accumulated. At 1-3 tokens, choose to either lose 1 Hope or mark 1 Stress. At 4-5 tokens, choose to either give the GM 1 Fear or lose 1 HP. At 6-7 tokens, the character suffers both tiers—choose one from Hope/Stress AND one from Fear/HP. At 8 or more tokens, the character is consumed by the mist and lost forever. The player must create a new character.

This escalating pressure creates natural story arcs where characters must find ways to cleanse their corruption before it reaches fatal levels.

**Mist Tokens (Quick Reference):**

**Gaining Mist Tokens:**
- **Resting in Mist-Touched locations**: 2 tokens (short rest), 3 tokens (long rest)
- **Adversary abilities**: Certain mist creatures inflict Mist tokens through attacks/abilities
- **GM Fear spending**: 3 Fear → all party members gain 1 token (2 Fear when off-Path)
- **NOT from**: Simply entering or traveling through Mist-Touched locations

**Mist Token Corruption Effects (Condition):**

When you gain a Mist token, immediately suffer consequences based on your total:

- **1-3 Tokens**: Choose one: Lose 1 Hope OR mark 1 Stress
- **4-5 Tokens**: Choose one: GM gains 1 Fear OR lose 1 HP
- **6-7 Tokens**: Suffer both tiers (choose one from each: Hope/Stress AND Fear/HP)
- **8+ Tokens**: Your character is consumed by the mist and lost. Create a new character.




**Mist Mechanics:**
- **Spend 2 Fear (Session Start)** → Make one location on the Path Mist-Touched
- **Spend 3 Fear (Mid-Session)** → Make currently Unstable location Mist-Touched
- **Spend 3 Fear (When in Mist Touched Location)** → Every party member gains 1 Mist token
- **Spend 2 Fear (when off-Path)** → Every party member gains 1 Mist token
